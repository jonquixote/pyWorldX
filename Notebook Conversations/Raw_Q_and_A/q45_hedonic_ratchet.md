**Question:** How does the 'Desired Standard of Living' equation behave under scarcity? If wealth previously rose, expectations rise. Can the model mathematically capture the 'Hedonic Ratchet' effect, where populations refuse to lower their industrial consumption until physical EROI shortages forcefully cut them off?

---

Answer:
**The mathematical models capture the "Hedonic Ratchet" effect through delayed 
expectation variables and progress-rate feedback loops, demonstrating that 
societies rarely lower their consumption voluntarily and are instead 
mechanically forced into contraction by thermodynamic limits.**

Here is how the 'Desired Standard of Living' and human expectations behave 
mathematically under scarcity within these biophysical models:

**1. "Adaptive Needs" and the Income Expectation Delay**
Modern agent-based additions to these models, such as the Needs and Limits (N&L)
framework, explicitly describe human needs and expectations as **"satiable, 
adaptive, and interdependent"** [1]. In the World3 architecture, the "Hedonic 
Ratchet" is mathematically represented by parameters like the **"income 
expectation averaging time"** [2, 3]. Because human expectations are calculated 
as a delayed rolling average of past income and wealth, a society that has 
experienced decades of exponential growth will possess extremely high, rigid 
expectations. When actual wealth generation begins to stagnate, the delayed 
expectation variable remains artificially high, preventing the population from 
voluntarily lowering its desired standard of living.

**2. The "Rate of Progress" and Social Tension**
In the updated Earth4All framework, this psychological ratchet effect is 
explicitly wired into a **"Progress reinforcing loop"** [4]. The model tracks 
the "observed rate of progress in wellbeing" [5]. 
* If people feel their standard of living is continually improving, social 
tension falls [6]. 
* However, if the rate of progress merely stagnates—even at a high level of 
absolute wealth—or if people feel they are falling behind elites, the model 
generates massive **"social tension"** [4, 6]. 
Because political decision-making is dominated by "short-termism" [7] and a 
conventional economic paradigm that dictates **"More is always better"** [8], 
populations and governments will fiercely resist reducing industrial consumption
to avoid triggering this social instability. 

**3. The Unchecked Industrial Feedback Loop**
Because society refuses to scale back its expectations, the **"unchecked 
industrial feedback loop"** continues to drive exponential demand for material 
wealth [9, 10]. In the Business-as-Usual (BAU) scenarios, the system attempts to
satisfy this entrenched demand by forcing the industrial sector to produce more 
goods and services, regardless of the underlying resource scarcity [9]. 

**4. The Forced Cut-Off: EROI and the 65% Energy Ceiling**
Since the "Desired Standard of Living" equation refuses to lower consumption 
organically, the system inevitably collides with hard physics. To meet the 
relentless demand, the industrial sector must extract increasingly dilute, 
high-entropy resources. 
* This causes the **Fraction of Capital Allocated to Obtaining Resources 
(FCAOR)** to skyrocket [11, 12]. 
* As the **Energy Return on Energy Invested (EROI)** collapses, extracting these
resources demands an exponential increase in physical energy and capital [11, 
13].
* The system is only forcefully cut off when it hits the **65% Thermodynamic 
Energy Ceiling**—the point where resource extraction demands more than 65% of 
the total global energy supply, triggering programmatic supply contractions [13,
14].

Ultimately, the RK4 arrays mathematically prove that because human expectations 
adapt upwards and resist contraction, the system will cannibalize itself to 
sustain the illusion of growth. The population's industrial consumption is only 
choked off when the **Maintenance Gap** causes physical depreciation to exceed 
available investment, forcing a sudden and uncontrollable collapse of the 
industrial base [12, 15, 16].

**Question:** As EROI collapses, the system requires more capital for resource extraction. Does the literature describe a 'Bifurcation of Access', where the bottom 50% loses access to commercial energy completely while the top 10% maintains it? How is this mathematically modeled in the Capital Flow?

---

Answer:
As the **Energy Return on Investment (EROI)** collapses, the literature 
identifies a mechanical shift where the system must divert an escalating 
fraction of its industrial capital toward resource extraction to maintain 
physical flows [1, 2]. This "capital squeeze" triggers what the sources describe
as **accentuated inequalities** and a potential "bifurcation" where a privileged
minority maintains access to services and energy while the majority experiences 
a collapse in living standards [3, 4].

### 1. The 'Social Suicide' and Bifurcation Mechanism
While the original World3 assumes a global average for distribution, the 
literature and recent updates explicitly address the fallacy of equal 
distribution during a resource crisis.

*   **The Survival Threshold:** The sources note that as total resources 
diminish, inequalities are "accentuated" [3]. Crucially, they describe a 
**"Social Suicide" threshold**: equal sharing is abandoned when the average 
resource amount per person is no longer enough to maintain life [3]. 
*   **The Interpretation of Scenarios:** Recent analysis (Herrington 2021) 
suggests that the world may experience a **split-scenario future**. In this 
view, the "top 10%" (the rich) may experience a **Comprehensive Technology 
(CT)** trajectory—using high-cost innovation to maintain welfare—while the 
"bottom 50%" (the poor) bear the brunt of a **Business-as-Usual (BAU)** collapse
[4, 5].
*   **Resource Priority:** Resources are diverted to service a "smaller fraction
of the population only," as maintaining the entire global population's standard 
of living becomes "uneconomic" under conditions of high extraction costs [6].

### 2. Mathematical Modeling in the Capital Flow
To represent this bifurcation in the **Capital Flow** and **Market Mechanisms**,
advanced biophysical models (like **WORLD7** and the planned **World4/PEEC**) 
implement the following mathematical logic:

*   **Willingness to Pay (WTP) Gating:** In the `FinanceSector`, access to 
commercial energy is gated by **endogenous market prices** [7, 8]. As extraction
costs spike due to declining ore grades or EROI, the market price rises to keep 
the supply chain profitable [1, 8]. Access is then programmatically determined 
by the **"Ability to Pay"** of different cohorts. If a cohort's disposable 
income falls below the price of subsistence energy/food, their demand is 
"modified" (reduced) to zero in the integration step [9].
*   **The 'Success to the Successful' Loop:** The literature identifies an 
internal feedback loop where privileged populations have an easier time saving 
and multiplying capital [10, 11]. Mathematically, this is modeled by splitting 
the population into **"Owners" and "Workers"** cohorts [12]. Owners maintain 
high **Liquid Funds** and can afford the "accumulated cost of technologies" 
needed to bypass limits, while Workers' income is entirely consumed by rising 
subsistence costs [13, 14].
*   **Non-Discretionary Spending (TNDS):** In the capital allocation array, a 
rising fraction of GDP is dedicated to **unavoidable expenditure** (maintenance 
of depleted flows) [15, 16]. When the **Incurred Cost of Maintenance** exceeds a
region or cohort's wealth generation, the model triggers a "Minsky Moment" where
debt can no longer be serviced, and capital flows to that node's service sector 
are programmatically cut [17, 18].
*   **Thermodynamic Ceiling Priority:** The **65% Energy Ceiling** enforces a 
hard physical cap [19]. If total energy demand for extraction exceeds this 
limit, the `CentralRegistrar` must prioritize flows. In regionalized or 
class-split models, capital and energy are programmatically directed toward the 
nodes with higher **Security Value**, leaving the "periphery" (the bottom 50%) 
to face a supply collapse [17, 20].

In summary, the literature describes a system that maintains complexity for a 
subset of the population by **externalizing the costs of EROI collapse** onto 
the majority, using market price signals and stratified capital pools to manage 
the resulting biophysical shortages [4, 5].

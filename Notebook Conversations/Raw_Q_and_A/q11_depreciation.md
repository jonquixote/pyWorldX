In a modern Python ODE loop for the `FinanceSector`, the literature—specifically

Under conditions of extreme energy scarcity, depreciation **accelerates

### 1. Mathematical Modeling of Depreciation vs. Maintenance
In the original World3, depreciation was a simple linear discard rate: 
$Depreciation = IC / ALIC$ (Average Lifetime of Industrial Capital) [3]. To 
modernize this in `pyWorldX`, you must treat depreciation as **physical wear** 
that requires an offsetting **Maintenance Flux**.

*   **The Required Maintenance ($M_{req}$):** This is the capital investment 
needed annually to keep the current stock of complex structures (factories, 
energy grids, etc.) from decaying [1].
*   **The Maintenance Gap:** In your ODE loop, if the energy cost of resource 
extraction rises exponentially (due to declining ore grades or EROI), the 
system's "Liquid Funds" are drained [4-6].
*   **The Trigger:** When **Maintenance Investment ($M_{inv}$)** falls below 
**Required Maintenance ($M_{req}$)**, the physical decay of the stock 
accelerates [2].

### 2. Non-Linear Acceleration via the 'Decay Multiplier'
The literature suggests that depreciation accelerates non-linearly through a 
"broad-front systemic collapse" mechanism once the maintenance backlog catches 
up [2].

**The ODE Equation Logic:**
$$\frac{dIC}{dt} = \text{Investment} - ( \text{BaseDepreciation} \times 
\phi(\text{MaintenanceRatio}) )$$
Where:
*   **BaseDepreciation:** The standard aging process ($IC / ALIC_{norm}$) [3, 
7].
*   **MaintenanceRatio:** $\frac{\text{Actual Maintenance 
Investment}}{\text{Required Maintenance Cost}}$.
*   **The Non-linear Multiplier ($\phi$):** A lookup table or exponential 
function where $\phi = 1.0$ if the ratio is $\geq 1.0$, but spikes exponentially
(e.g., to $2.0$ or $4.0$) as the ratio drops toward zero [1, 2].

### 3. Impact of Extreme Energy Scarcity
Energy is identified as the "prerequisite" for maintaining infrastructure [8]. 
The sources outline a specific sequence that leads to non-linear depreciation 
spikes:
1.  **Energy Squeeze:** As EROI falls, the system must divert more capital to 
resource extraction (FCAOR) just to maintain energy flows [9, 10].
2.  **Investment Starvation:** This leaves less capital for "citizen services 
and industrial re-investment" [10].
3.  **The Maintenance Backlog:** Society temporarily offsets deficits with 
loans, but eventually reaches a **150% Debt-to-GDP limit** [2, 11].
4.  **The Unraveling:** Once the "Security Value" of the capital assets is 
exceeded by debt, the system loses **financial resilience** [2]. At this "Minsky
Moment," maintenance investment collapses to near zero, causing the physical 
infrastructure to "unravel" and depreciate at rates far higher than their design
specifications [2, 12].

**Question:** What is the correct functional form for the heat shock multiplier on agricultural yields as a function of temperature anomaly? In our model, the multiplier is 1.0 at T=0 and drops to 0.0 at some critical threshold. Should this be a linear decline, or is there a non-linear response with wet-bulb temperature thresholds, crop-specific sensitivities, or CO2 fertilization offsets? What are the critical temperature thresholds for major crop failures?

---

Answer:
Answer:
The heat shock multiplier on agricultural yields must be modeled as an **extreme, non-linear 
tipping point**, rather than a simple linear decline [1, 2]. 

Here is how the literature specifies the functional form, offsets, and thresholds for your 
model:

**1. The Non-Linear 35°C Threshold**
The critical local temperature threshold for major crop failures is **35°C** [1, 2]. Pushing 
crops above this thermal limit results in a "heat shock" that triggers a dramatic, precipitous, 
and non-linear drop in agricultural yields [2]. *(Note: While a 35°C wet-bulb temperature is 
specifically identified in the literature as the absolute biophysical limit for human 
survivability, the ~35°C threshold applied directly to crops is enough to trigger catastrophic 
agricultural failure regardless of human labor limits [2, 3]).*

**2. CO₂ Fertilization Offsets**
The functional form does not drop instantly because it is buffered by **CO₂ fertilization 
offsets**, which introduce a competing dynamic. 
*   Increased atmospheric CO₂ creates a "growth enhancing fertilization effect" that initially 
counteracts the "growth reducing effect" of higher temperatures [4]. 
*   In the early stages of warming (e.g., between +0.7°C and +2.0°C), this fertilization effect 
is stronger, meaning global yield reductions might initially be limited to under 10% [4]. 
*   However, as temperatures continue to escalate, this offset is overwhelmed. The **"excess 
heat takes over,"** and the negative, non-linear impacts of thermal stress strictly dominate 
[4]. 

**3. Regional and Crop-Specific Sensitivities**
Your multiplier cannot be applied as a uniform global average, as the functional response is 
highly dependent on region and crop type:
*   **Regional Variation:** Northern latitudes may actually see temporary growth stimulation 
from combined warming and CO₂, whereas tropical countries are already experiencing substantial, 
economically damaging yield reductions from heat shock [2, 4, 5]. 
*   **Crop-Specific Vulnerabilities:** The non-linear shock is especially threatening to a core 
set of globally traded grains—**wheat, maize (corn), soy, and rice**—which provide approximately
30% of human protein [6, 7]. 

**4. The Risk of Simultaneous Breadbasket Failure**
Finally, the mathematical form must account for systemic network vulnerabilities. Escalating 
climate change increases the probability of **simultaneous harvest failures** across major 
global breadbaskets (such as North America, Europe, and Australia) [8, 9]. Driven by complex 
climate events like a strongly meandering jet stream, these concurrent extremes mean your 
multiplier should allow for synchronized, multi-regional yield collapses that bypass local 
buffering and directly threaten the global food supply [8, 9].
